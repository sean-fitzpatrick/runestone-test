Let \(V,W\) be vector spaces. Let \(B=\{\mathbf{b}_1,\ldots, \mathbf{b}_n\}\) be a basis of \(V\text{,}\) and let \(\ww_1,\ldots, \ww_n\) be any vectors in \(W\text{.}\) (These vectors need not be distinct.) Then there exists a unique linear transformation \(T:V\to W\) such that \(T(\mathbf{b}_i)=\ww_i\) for each \(i=1,2,\ldots, n\text{;}\) indeed, we can define \(T\) as follows: given \(\vv\in V\text{,}\) write \(\vv=c_1\vv_1+\cdots +c_n\vv_n\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} T(\vv)=T(c_1\vv_1+\cdots + c_n\vv_n) = c_1\ww_1+\cdots +c_n\ww_n\text{.} \end{equation*}
