Exercise 1.2.4.

Rearrange the blocks to create a valid proof of the following statement:

If \(c\vv=\zer\text{,}\) then either \(c=0\) or \(\vv=\zer\text{.}\)

Let \(c\) be a scalar, and let \(\vv\in V\) be a vector.
 
Suppose that \(c\vv=\zer\text{.}\)

---

By the law of the excluded middle, either \(c=0\text{,}\) or \(c\neq 0\text{.}\)

---

If \(c=0\text{,}\) then \(c=0\) or \(\vv=\zer\text{,}\) and we're done.

---

Suppose then that \(c\neq 0\text{.}\)

---

Since \(c\neq 0\text{,}\) there is a scalar \(1/c\) such that \((1/c)\cdot c=1\text{.}\)

---

Since \(c\vv=\zer\text{,}\) \((1/c)(c\vv)=(1/c)\zer\text{.}\)

---

Therefore, \(((1/c)\cdot c)\vv = \zer\text{,}\) using S4 and Part b of Exercise 1.2.2.

---

Since \((1/c)\cdot c=1\text{,}\) we have \(1\vv=\vv =\zer\text{,}\) using S5.

---

Since \(\vv=\zer\text{,}\) \(c=0\) or \(\vv=\zer\text{.}\)

---

In either case, we conclude that \(c=0\) or \(\vv=\zer\text{,}\) so the result is proven.
